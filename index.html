FCS 2026

2026 Workshop on Foundations of Computer Security

July 25, 2026 – Lisbon, Portugal

Affiliated with FLoC 2026

Background, Aim and Scope

Computer security is an established field of both theoretical and practical significance. In recent years, there has been sustained interest in the formal foundations of methods used in computer security. The goals of the 2026 Foundations of Computer Security workshop (FCS 2026) are twofold. First, we aim to provide a forum for the discussion of continued research in this area. Second, we wish to support young researchers in developing long-term connections in the research community and provide them with access to perspectives from researchers from other institutions.

To support the first goal, FCS 2026 welcomes papers on all topics related to the formal underpinnings of security and privacy, and their applications. FCS 2026 is interested in new theoretical results, in exploratory presentations that examine open questions and raise fundamental concerns about existing theories, and in the development of security/privacy tools using formal techniques. Demonstrations of tools based on formal techniques are welcome, as long as the demonstrations can be carried out on a standard digital projector (i.e., without any specialized equipment). We solicit the submission of both mature work and work in progress.

To support the second goal, FCS 2026 will follow the lead of larger conferences and our own past success and provide attendees with mentoring opportunities alongside the research track. In particular, there will be multiple mentoring sessions throughout the day so junior researchers can meet and interact with their senior counterparts.

Program

Invited Speaker


Jingbo Wang
Purdue University

Title: From Alarms to Evidence: Probabilistic Datalog for Security Analysis

Abstract. Static analysis is a standard tool for finding security vulnerabilities, but its guarantees come with a cost. Such analyses are often sound but not complete: they over-approximate program behavior and may report alarms that cannot occur in any concrete execution. In this talk, I will describe a line of work that uses probabilistic Datalog to attach quantitative meaning to these alarms. The key idea is to treat uncertain analysis facts probabilistically and perform inference over the resulting Datalog derivations. This makes it possible to estimate the likelihood of reported queries, identify conflicts among them, update results incrementally when inputs change, and model dependencies among program facts more precisely. I will discuss these ideas in the context of several security-relevant analyses, including side-channel analysis, taint analysis, disassembly analysis, and data-race analysis.

Submission

FCS 2026 welcomes two kinds of submissions:

  • full papers (at most 12 pages, excluding references and well-marked appendices)
  • short papers (at most 2 pages, excluding references and well-marked appendices)

FCS 2026 will employ a light form of double-blind reviewing. Submitted papers must (a) omit any reference to the authors' names or the names of their institutions, and (b) reference the authors' own related work in the third person (e.g., not "We build on our previous work ..." but rather "We build on the work of ..."). Nothing should be done in the name of anonymity that weakens the submission or makes the job of reviewing the paper more difficult (e.g., important background references should not be omitted or anonymized). The author information will be revealed to the reviewers only after papers have been accepted. If you have questions, please see the CSF 2018 FAQ page. When in doubt, contact the program chairs.

All submissions will be peer-reviewed by the program committee listed below. Authors of accepted papers must guarantee that their papers will be presented at the workshop. Short papers will receive the same rigorous reviews as full papers, but might receive a shorter talk slot at the workshop, depending on the number of accepted submissions.

Papers may be formatted using a two-column proceedings style accepted by IEEE, ACM, or USENIX conferences. The first page should include the paper's title, an abstract, and a list of keywords. Committee members are not required to read appendices, so papers must be intelligible without them. Papers not adhering to the page limits may be rejected without consideration of their merits.

Paper submissions will be done through HotCRP in the PDF format. The submission website is here.

Informal Proceedings

The workshop has no published proceedings. Presenting a paper at the workshop should not preclude submission to or publication in other venues (before, after, or concurrently with FCS 2026). Papers presented at the workshop will be made available to workshop participants, but this does not constitute an official proceedings.
